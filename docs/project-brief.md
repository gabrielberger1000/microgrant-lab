# Project Brief

## One-Sentence Description

Microgrant Lab is an experimental AI-assisted workflow for reviewing small emergency grants, beginning with utility shutoff prevention grants capped at $300.

## Core Question

Can an AI-assisted review process make small-grant decisions faster, cheaper, more consistent, and more auditable without creating unacceptable fraud, fairness, privacy, or legitimacy failures?

## Starting Hypothesis

Small emergency grants are unusually compatible with AI-assisted review because the downside is bounded, the evidence is often documentable, review-cost tolerance is low, and outcomes can be measured more concretely than in broad hardship programs.

The first useful breakthrough is not full automation. It is a tight human-in-the-loop workflow where AI helps with:

- applicant interviewing,
- evidence normalization,
- case summarization,
- inconsistency detection,
- follow-up question generation,
- policy checklists,
- audit trails,
- adversarial testing.

## What This Is

- A research and infrastructure lab for narrow microgrant workflows.
- A synthetic-data environment for testing policy, fraud, and fairness failures.
- A prototype decision-support system for overloaded reviewers.
- A possible foundation for future nonprofit, foundation, or mutual-aid partnerships.

## What This Is Not

- Not a public charity launch.
- Not an autonomous grantmaking system.
- Not a general poverty classifier.
- Not a donor-facing fundraising product.
- Not a replacement for social workers, case managers, or community organizations.
- Not ready for real applicant data until the privacy, security, and review workflow are defined.

## Initial Pilot

The initial pilot category is:

**Utility Shutoff Prevention Under $300**

This category is useful as a first test because:

- the grant cap is small,
- the evidence is documentable,
- the applicant need is urgent and concrete,
- payment can be routed to a utility vendor,
- the outcome is measurable,
- the policy can be written narrowly.

## Operating Principles

1. **Assistive before autonomous**  
   AI may recommend, summarize, and flag, but a human owns the decision.

2. **Narrow policy beats broad empathy**  
   The system should do one constrained workflow well before expanding.

3. **Auditability is a product feature**  
   Every recommendation should be traceable to evidence, policy checks, and known uncertainty.

4. **Fraud testing starts immediately**  
   Synthetic adversaries and red-team cases are part of the baseline, not a later add-on.

5. **Legitimacy matters as much as accuracy**  
   The workflow must be explainable to applicants, reviewers, partners, and funders.

6. **Minimize sensitive data**  
   Collect only what is needed for the pilot decision and verification route.

## Near-Term Milestones

1. Define the first pilot policy and decision statuses.
2. Create structured case and decision schemas.
3. Generate synthetic normal, borderline, and adversarial cases.
4. Build a baseline policy evaluator.
5. Add AI case summarization and reviewer-assist prompts.
6. Run volunteer or partner cases in shadow mode.
7. Publish a first internal transparency report.
